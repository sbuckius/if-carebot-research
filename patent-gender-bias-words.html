So… can we trust these results?
On AI, rules, and the lure of false precision
1
AI doesn't read context — it matches patterns. When it labels "secured" as masculine-coded, it's not reasoning about a 19th-century sewing patent. It's firing a rule learned from job ad research where words like "competitive" and "dominant" correlated with male applicants. That research is real. Whether it applies to an 1882 spool case description is… less clear.
2
The original research wasn't about patents. Gaucher et al. (2011) studied job advertisements and how certain word choices affected who applied. It found real, measurable effects. But AI has abstracted those findings into a general rule — "simple = feminine, mechanism = masculine" — and applied it universally, including to technical patent text that was never part of the original study.
3
The labels can reinforce the biases they claim to identify. Calling "comfortable" feminine and "actuated" masculine sounds like analysis — but it's also a description of what we already expect gender to look like. It can make those expectations feel objective, scientific, confirmed. That's the lure of translating messy social reality into tidy rules.
4
And yet — something real is happening here. Even with all these caveats, the pattern holds: every patent in this dataset uses language from both columns. The women who wrote these patents weren't writing in the "feminine" register that gender expectations might predict. That finding doesn't need AI to be true. The words are right there in the original documents.
✦ The finding — despite all of the above
Every single patent by a woman used both masculine-coded and feminine-coded language.
AI's classifications are flawed, context-blind, and sometimes absurd. But even by its own imperfect rules, these women's patents refuse to be sorted into a single gender register. The language did whatever the invention needed. The rules couldn't keep up.
