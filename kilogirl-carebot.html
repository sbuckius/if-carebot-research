✦ Critical Context · Read Before Asking
⚠️ He coined the unit "kilo-girl hours" to measure the labour of women astronomers he did not name.
Harlow Shapley's work on globular clusters helped reshape our understanding of the Milky Way's size and our place in it. When asked who helped him, he said the work was done with Gretchen and "maybe one or two assistants." He introduced the term "girl hours" as a unit of measurement for their labour — and noted that one job alone took several kilo-girl hours. A kilo-girl hour is one thousand woman-hours of work. The women who produced these measurements were called "computers" — their job was to calculate, measure, and process data by hand. Harvard Observatory was "swarming with these assistants," Shapley said. That is why, he explained, they got somewhere. The women made the science possible. They are almost entirely absent from the historical record by name.
✦ Oral History Transcript · Harlow Shapley Interview
Weiner:
You talk of the work on globular clusters and the revolutionary implications of it which you pursued and published. And you talked of "we." Now, was this a solo effort or were there others involved with you in a discussion of this?
Shapley:
The discussion mostly was with Gretchen and maybe one or two assistants. You see, it took a tremendous lot of measures. I introduced the term "girl hours," and it would take a lot of girl hours to solve some of the problems. In fact, one job took several kilo-girl hours to get it through. So I should say that I had help on a lot of the details. But I don't think I had much help in the hunching.
Weiner:
This was at Mount Wilson?
Shapley:
Both places. But not many girl hours at Mount Wilson. Very few. I had one full time girl assistant as I got along, but there were very few at Mount Wilson whereas Harvard was just swarming with these assistants. That's why we got somewhere, got it done.
