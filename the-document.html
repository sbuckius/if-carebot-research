What This Document Is and How It Was Made

This document was generated by AI. It was produced by prompting a large language model to examine the 1969 Neiman-Marcus Kitchen Computer advertisement photograph — featuring the Honeywell H316 — and to analyze the visible physical and emotional work being performed by the person in the image. The AI was asked to render that analysis as executable code: a program running on a human body.

The term "presentation labor" is used here in the sense developed by Sarah ████████ to describe the measurable, structured, and often gendered work of constructing and sustaining a particular self-presentation for an audience. The document is the AI's attempt to make that labor legible — to write the program that the image was designed to conceal.

The model producing this document was almost certainly trained on foundational scholarship in this area. The most likely sources are listed in the Sources section below. This document does not claim scientific validity. It is an artifact: evidence of what an AI sees when it looks at a body performing work, and of what language it reaches for to describe that seeing. The program is not the person. The program ran on the person. The person ran the program.

✦ Sources & Likely Training Material
Works the AI Was Probably Trained On
1
The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life
Erving Goffman · Doubleday Anchor Books, 1959
The foundational text for understanding social interaction as performance — front stage/backstage, impression management, sincere vs. cynical performance. The entire architecture of this document (a program that produces an appearance distinct from its internal state) is Goffman's dramaturgical model rendered as code.
2
The Managed Heart: Commercialization of Human Feeling
Arlie Russell Hochschild · University of California Press, 1983
Coined "emotional labor." Module 7's Emotion_Conversion_Matrix is a direct formalization of Hochschild's conversion model: the table mapping INPUT_STATE to OUTPUT_STATE is exactly what she described. The smile is not her smile.
3
Aesthetic Labour
Chris Warhurst & Dennis Nickson · SAGE, 2020 · Original concept: Warhurst, Nickson, Witz & Cullen (2000)
Developed "aesthetic labor" — the mobilization and commodification of embodied capacities to look good and sound right. Modules 9–11 (apron, garments, hair) and Module 23 (class performance) draw directly on this. The apron costs $28. Her aesthetic labor is not listed.
4
Aesthetic Labour for the Sociologies of Work, Gender, and Beauty
Ashley Mears · Sociology Compass, 2014
Bridges Hochschild's emotional labor with Bourdieu's aesthetic capital through gender and beauty — situating bodily appearance as a convertible social resource. Informs Module 20 (the body as advertisement for the advertisement) and Module 23.
5
Emotional Labor Since The Managed Heart
Ronnie J. Steinberg & Deborah M. Figart · The Annals of the American Academy, 1999
A comprehensive survey of the emotional labor literature in the decades following Hochschild. Particularly relevant to the document's treatment of gendered labor and the disproportionate burden of presentation labor on women.
6
The Beauty Myth: How Images of Beauty Are Used Against Women
Naomi Wolf · William Morrow, 1991
Analyzes beauty standards as a mechanism for social control and economic exclusion. Module 35 (the advertisement frame as pressure system) and the closing declaration on unlisted prices map directly onto Wolf's thesis: her body is the condition under which the product becomes legible as desirable.
7
Aesthetic Labour in Interactive Service Work
Warhurst, Nickson, Witz & Cullen · Service Industries Journal, 2000
The paper that coined "aesthetic labour" as a distinct concept. The document's MAINTAIN and CORRECTION instructions throughout are a formalization of this managerial aesthetic demand rendered as self-directed code.
